A Factual Revenue is Hard to Find

Posted on Wed 26 September 2018 in posts

A Factual Revenue is Hard to Find¶

The publicly traded companies in the U.S. present their information according to Generally Accepted Accounting Principles (GAAP) in their annual or quartely financial reports and shared with the shareholders and Security Exchange Commission (SEC). These financial reportings are an important source of financial information for investors to obtain and evaluate a company's current and even future performance. Since 2010, the reportings are also presented in a standard reporting format XBRL eXtensible Business Reporting Language and these XBRL financial reporting data can be freely accessed on the SEC website and recently in Google BigQuery. The standardized reporting format is an effort to provide an ease and unified access for analysts to obtain financial information from the financial reportings. This blog is an attempt to explore the accessibilty of these standard reporting dataset through the process of obtaining one of the important company's performace factor- Revenue. (Along the jurney of finding revenues, althouth I have learned a lot of basic financial reporting knowledge, I have not experienced an 'Ah' monment of the joy of discoverying I was looking for, instead it's the title of Flannery O'Connor's book "A good man is hard to find" that echoed in the back of my mind, hence goes the title ...)

The Problems in Finding Revenues¶

At first glance, it seems easy to locate the revenue number since every number in XBRL filings is tagged with a specific name. So to find the revenues, one only needs to find the numbers with the specific revenue tags. But the question is what tags are revenveue tags? Is there any standard tag for revenues? Well, yes and no. The us-gaap taxonomy does provide serveral tags for tagging revenues, the popular ones are "Revenues", "SalesRevenueNet" and "SalesRevenueGoodsNet" etc. But if you only use these tags to locate revenues, you would soon find out that you can't find revenues in many company filings. So in an attempt to leverage as many varieties of filings as possible, I tried to find tags containing the word 'revenue'. This approach does broaden the searching scope. However, at one extreme, it includes many undesired tags(false positive) and at the other extreme, it still misses revenues in many filings. This is because in some filings the revenues are not tagged with tags containning "revenue" at all. For example, many finanical institutions use the tag "InterestAndDividendIncomeOperating". To further complicate the situations, some companies do not even use the tags from the standard US-GAAP taxonomy, instead they use the companies specific customized tags, for example "RevenuesOperatingAndNonoperating", "Commissions" etc. So does there exist a systematic method to locate revenves in XBRL filings at all? To investigate the problem, I decided to turn to the domain knowledge. After reading a couple of books about finanical reporting[1] [2] , I come up with a solution that leverages the common reporting patterns.

The Revenue Reporting Patterns¶

Revenue is commonly reported as the first line item in the income statement of a company's financial report, and in the case of revenue being generated from several different sources(i.e. service vs. products), multiple line items may be disclosed respectively in a revenue group, and the sum of all these line items is reported as the last line of the group.

Finding Revenues in Financial Reportings¶

Once we get the tags for revenues, the next step is to use the obtained revenue tag list to retrieve the revenue numbers from company's XBRL financial reporting data. All public company's reportings are available on the SEC Financial Statements and Notes website, and Google BigQuery. To demonstrate the use of the revenue tags, I have simply downloaded the reporting data from 2016-Q3 to 2018-Q1 from the SEC website, and retrieved all company's 2016 revenues as shown in the script below,

In [ ]:
def get_revs(dirname, rev_tags):
    data_path = pathlib.Path.cwd().joinpath(pathlib.Path.cwd().parent, 'data','statements', dirname)

    num = pd.read_csv(data_path.joinpath('num.txt'), sep='\t', encoding='iso--8859-1', dtype={'coreg': str})
    sub = pd.read_csv(data_path.joinpath('sub.txt'), sep='\t', encoding='iso-8859-1', parse_dates=['period'], 
                        usecols=['adsh', 'cik', 'name', 'sic', 'countryba', 'stprba', 'cityba', 'zipba', 'stprinc', 'afs', 
                                 'fye', 'form', 'period', 'fy', 'fp', 'filed','nciks'])
    
    
    num['ddate'] = pd.to_datetime(num['ddate'], format="%Y%m%d", errors = 'coerce')
    sub['filed'] = pd.to_datetime(sub['filed'], format="%Y%m%d", errors = 'coerce')
    
    df_num_sub = num.merge(sub, on='adsh', how='inner')
    
    df_rev_all = df_num_sub.loc[(df_num_sub['tag'].isin(rev_tags)) 
              & (df_num_sub['qtrs'] == 4)
              & (df_num_sub['fp'] == 'FY')
              & (df_num_sub['ddate'].dt.year == 2016)
              & (df_num_sub['fy'].isin([2016, 2017]))
              & (df_num_sub['coreg'].isnull()), :]
    
    
    # gets the index of the maximum values for each reporting
    idxmax = df_rev_all.groupby('adsh').value.idxmax()
    
    # for a group with all 'value' being 0, the idxmax is null, so we need to drop NAs
    idxmax.dropna(axis=0, inplace=True)
    df_rev_max = df_rev_all.loc[idxmax, :]
    
    return df_rev_max

Verify the Resulting Revenues¶

Now we got all company's revenue numbers, let's see how well this approach works. To verify the resulting data, I have compaired it against the total revenues for S&P; 500 companies from New York Stock Exchange dataset. Out of the 206 available revenue numbers dated with the year 2016 from the dataset, there are only 2 cases that show the possible problems with the resulting revenues derived from this approach. The problem arises when a filing reports revenues with different tags in its financial statement of income and detailed notes. For example, one of the two cases is the filing from the company USB(US BANCORP \DE). In the income statement, the revenue is reported with the tag 'InterestAndDividendIncomeOperating', and in the detailed notes, there is also a number with different value taged with "Revenues". With this approach, the larger number with the tag "Revenues" are chosen as the company's revenue. It is not clear to me why the revenue number in the income statement is different from the number in the notes detail. However, if the filing is to be trusted, this case calls for a more refined approach for finding revenues from filings. The merely using tag approach is not sufficient, the location where the tag appears should also be taken into consideration. That is finding revenues with tags only appearing in income statements.

All in all, the discovered tags with the reporting patterns work quite well in almost all cases.
